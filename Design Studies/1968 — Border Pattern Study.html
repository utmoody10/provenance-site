01 / Anatomy of the Mark

What is the individual element?

Before studying the pattern as a whole, it is necessary to isolate and understand the single repeating unit — the mark from which everything else is built.

Isolated Mark — 40× Magnification
A single mark from the 1968 border rendered at extreme enlargement. Note: the edges are not perfectly clean. The mark has a slight feathering on two sides — a consequence of ink spread on uncoated card stock. The corners are not sharp. The fill is not uniform.
Mark Geometry — Annotated
The mark is approximately 3.2:1 width-to-height ratio. Slight angle deviation of 2–6° from horizontal in each occurrence. Ink density is highest at center, fading toward edges. This fading is what gives the pattern its warmth and depth.
Shape
A short, fat rectangle with very slightly rounded corners — not a dash, not a brick, not a pill. Something between all three. Its horizontality is deliberate: it reads as a surface weave, not a stack.
Proportion
Width-to-height approximately 3:1. This is the same proportion as a standard running stitch or a flat weave pick — the relationship between warp and weft in a plain weave textile. Not accidental.
Variation
No two marks in the border are identical. Each carries slight angle deviation (2–6°), slight size variation (±10%), and ink density variation. The system is consistent. The instances are not.
02 / The Pattern System

How the marks organize into a field

The border is not a random scatter. There is a loose underlying grid — but the marks violate it regularly enough that it disappears into texture. This is the key tension in the design.

The Underlying Grid
The invisible structure. Marks are placed at roughly equal intervals — but the grid is never enforced precisely.
Grid + Deviation
Each mark deviates from its grid position by up to 30% of the cell size. This is the source of the organic quality.
Final Pattern — Grid Hidden
The grid disappears entirely. What remains reads as texture, not geometry. The order is felt but not seen.
The Core Design Principle
The 1968 border achieves something rare: it is simultaneously structured and organic. The grid provides the density and coverage that makes the pattern feel intentional. The deviation provides the warmth and imperfection that makes it feel human. Remove the grid and it becomes noise. Remove the deviation and it becomes mechanical. The balance between the two is what makes this pattern timeless — and why it has never looked dated.
Too Rigid — Pattern becomes mechanical
Zero deviation. The grid is fully visible. This reads as a geometric print — not a texture. It has no warmth.
Too Random — Pattern loses structure
Maximum deviation. The grid is lost. The result is noise — no visual weight, no sense of surface, no rhythm.
03 / Scale Studies

How the pattern reads at different sizes

Scale is everything. The same pattern at different densities communicates completely differently — from visible texture to fine grain to solid tone. Each scale has a specific application.

Coarse — Large Mark
Readable as individual marks. Appropriate for large-scale print, jacquard, or debossed packaging.
Medium — Standard
Original card scale equivalent. Reads as texture at arm's length. Embroidery, woven label, printed interior tape.
Fine — Dense
Marks begin to merge visually. Approaches the appearance of a solid tone with surface grain. Ideal for fine fabric weave.
Micro — Grain
Resolves to near-solid with subtle depth. The structure is felt rather than seen. High-end screen print or laser etch.
04 / Color Studies

The pattern in the Provenance color system

The border pattern is color-neutral — it derives its warmth from color, not from form. Translated into the Provenance palette, the pattern shifts in register while retaining its structural identity.

Original — Amber on Buff
Source material. The amber mark on warm buff ground. High warmth, medium contrast.
Provenance — Navy on Cream
Primary brand application. Cooler register, higher contrast. More graphic than the original but maintains depth.
Provenance — Gold on Navy
Accent application. High drama. For interior details, packaging, and premium touch-points only.
Cream on Navy — Reversed
The inverse. Cream marks on navy ground. Reads as lighter, more airy. Works for interior lining or contrast details.
Tonal — Navy on Navy-Light
Near-tonal. The subtlest application. Structure felt rather than seen. Woven tone-on-tone fabric or debossed leather.
Gold on Cream — Warm Accent
Closest to the original's warmth translated into the brand palette. Interior label, tissue pattern, packaging print.
05 / Application Studies

Where this pattern lives on a garment

Six specific applications — not garment designs, but locations where the pattern logic would express itself. Each carries a different scale, color, and production method.

Collar Tape / Neck Binding Interior
The pattern at medium scale, navy on cream, on a narrow horizontal tape. Seen only when the collar is open or the garment is held inside-out. A quality signal that rewards inspection. Width: 12–16mm. Production: woven jacquard tape or screen-printed twill tape.
Woven Label — Oval Format
The border pattern as the background field of a woven label. The Provenance wordmark or P mark sits on top of the pattern. This references the original card directly — text over the textured field — without reproduction. Production: woven jacquard label.
Pocket Bag Interior
The pattern printed on the pocket bag fabric — visible only when the hand is in the pocket or the garment is fully examined. Gold on navy or gold on cream. A discovery detail. Production: rotary screen print on cotton batiste or voile lining fabric.
Placket Lining
The pattern on the interior face of the button placket — visible when the shirt is unbuttoned. Fine scale, navy on cream. The viewer experiences the pattern at close range, which rewards the fine detail. Production: woven or printed placket tape, self-fabric.

06 / Pattern as Brand Asset

Why this pattern belongs to Provenance

Central Observation
The 1968 border pattern is not a sports motif. It has no association with a team, a player, or a game. It is a pure design solution — a method of creating surface, warmth, and depth using a single repeated mark. As such, it is available to be claimed by a brand that understands what it actually is. Provenance can adopt the principle behind this pattern and make it entirely its own.
It is already warm
The pattern's amber-on-buff original colorway sits within the Provenance palette's register. Translated to navy-on-cream or gold-on-cream, it moves directly into the brand system without translation loss. The warmth is structural, not just coloristic.
It implies craft
The pattern's organic variation — its slight imperfection — reads as handmade even when it is not. In an era of perfect digital prints, this quality is rare and valuable. A pattern that appears to have been made by hand, even at industrial scale, signals a different standard of care.
It is scale-flexible
From a micro-weave in a fine fabric to a coarse print on packaging kraft, the pattern system holds its identity across an enormous range of scale. This is unusual. Most patterns become unrecognizable when scaled up or down by a factor of five. This one does not.
It has no direct origin
The pattern is not trademarked. It is a printing technique. The visual vocabulary it created — organic rectangular marks on a warm field — belongs to no one. It is available to be reinterpreted without restriction.
It connects generations
This pattern was experienced by multiple generations — often as a physical object held in the hand. It carries embedded memory. Used in a new context, it activates that memory without explaining itself. Recognition without reference.
It disappears into quality
At fine scales, in tonal colorways, the pattern is invisible as a pattern — it simply becomes surface quality. A fabric with this pattern woven in looks richer than a plain fabric. The design works hardest when it is least visible.
